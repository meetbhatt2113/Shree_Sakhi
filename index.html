Frequently Asked, Rarely Answered

The questions girls whisper to their friends at 1am — answered out loud, in plain language.

Late periods & pregnancy worry

Maybe — but a late period doesn't automatically mean pregnancy. Stress, sudden weight change, travel, illness, thyroid issues, PCOD, or even breastfeeding can delay a period by days or weeks. If it's been 7 days or more past your expected date, take a home urine pregnancy test first thing in the morning for the most accurate result — testing too early can give a false negative. If it's negative but your period still doesn't come within a week, or if you get a positive result, please see a gynecologist to confirm and plan next steps. Try not to spiral before you have the actual answer — one test will tell you more than a hundred worried thoughts.

A normal cycle is anywhere from 21–35 days, so a small shift month to month is not unusual. If your period is more than 7–10 days late, or if you regularly skip periods, it's worth getting checked — this can point to PCOD, thyroid imbalance, stress, or pregnancy. Track your cycle for 2–3 months (a simple calendar or app works) so a doctor has real data to work with.

Emergency contraception pills are most effective the sooner they're taken, ideally within 72 hours, though some types work up to 120 hours. They're available at most pharmacies without much delay. This is a backup option, not a regular birth control method, and it can shift your next period's timing — that's normal. If you're unsure which one to take or have questions after, a gynecologist or even a pharmacist can guide you without judgement.

PCOD / PCOS

PCOD/PCOS (Polycystic Ovarian Disease/Syndrome) is a hormonal condition where the ovaries produce slightly higher levels of male hormones (androgens), which can disrupt ovulation. It's extremely common — many young women have it and live full, healthy lives once it's managed. It is not something you did wrong, and it doesn't mean you can't have children.

Common signs include irregular or missed periods, excess facial/body hair, acne, sudden weight gain (especially around the belly), thinning hair on the scalp, and dark patches of skin on the neck or underarms. Having one or two of these doesn't confirm PCOD — a doctor usually checks through a pelvic ultrasound and blood tests (hormone levels) before diagnosing it.

There's no single "cure," but it's very manageable. Regular movement (30 minutes most days), a diet lower in refined sugar and processed food, consistent sleep, and stress management can noticeably improve symptoms. Some women also need medication from a gynecologist or endocrinologist to regulate cycles or hormones — that's completely normal and not a failure on your part.

PCOD can make ovulation irregular, which can make conceiving take longer — but the large majority of women with PCOD do go on to have healthy pregnancies, sometimes with a little medical support like ovulation-tracking or fertility medication. Getting it diagnosed and managed early makes the whole journey easier later.

Exercise & movement

Gentle, consistent movement is the goal — not intensity. Walking (20–30 minutes daily), prenatal yoga, pelvic floor exercises (kegels), and light stretching are generally considered safe through most of a healthy pregnancy. Avoid contact sports, heavy lifting, exercises lying flat on your back after the first trimester, and anything with a high fall risk. Always get your specific plan approved by your gynecologist, since it depends on your trimester and any complications.

Most healthy pregnancies benefit from 20–30 minutes of easy-paced walking a day, split into two shorter walks if that's more comfortable. Walk in the cooler parts of the day, wear supportive footwear, keep water with you, and stop immediately if you feel dizzy, breathless, or notice contractions or bleeding.

Food & diet

Focus on variety: iron-rich foods (leafy greens, dal, jaggery), calcium (milk, curd, sesame/til), protein (eggs, paneer, sprouts, lentils), fruits, and plenty of water. Your doctor will usually also prescribe folic acid, iron, and calcium supplements — take them as advised, food alone often isn't enough to meet pregnancy needs.

Reading, screen-time & mind

Anything that keeps you calm and smiling counts. Gentle fiction, light memoirs, or a good pregnancy/parenting book help you feel prepared without overwhelm. For screen time — comfort shows over intense thrillers or heavy news cycles tends to help mood and sleep. See our full Mind & Calm section below for specific picks.

Labour & delivery

Staying active throughout pregnancy (walking, prenatal yoga), maintaining a healthy weight gain as guided by your doctor, practising breathing techniques, and staying mentally prepared through antenatal classes are all linked to better chances of normal delivery. That said, birth doesn't always go to plan, and a C-section is sometimes the safest choice for you or the baby — that is never a failure, just a different, equally valid way to become a mother.

Regular, increasingly painful contractions that don't go away with rest, your water breaking, or a "bloody show" (mucus discharge tinged with blood) are common signs. If you're unsure, call your doctor or hospital — they'd always rather you call and be told it's early than wait too long.

When to worry

Heavy bleeding, severe abdominal pain, a bad headache with blurred vision, sudden swelling of the face/hands, reduced baby movement, fever, or fluid leaking — any of these need an urgent call to your doctor or hospital, not a wait-and-see approach.

First period (Menarche 101)

First — nothing is wrong with you, this means your body is healthy and growing exactly as it should. Periods usually start between ages 9–15. Use a pad, tampon, or menstrual cup, changing it every 4–6 hours. The first few cycles can be irregular, light, or heavy, and that's normal while your body finds its rhythm — it can take up to 1–2 years to settle into a pattern. Keep a small pouch with a spare pad in your bag, and it's okay to tell a trusted adult (mother, sister, teacher) — you don't have to figure this out alone.

It can be, since your body and hormones are still adjusting. A warm compress, gentle walking, and rest usually help. If the pain ever makes you faint, vomit repeatedly, or stops you from standing up, tell an adult and see a doctor — that level of pain isn't something to just "push through."

Menopause & perimenopause

Perimenopause is the transition phase before periods stop for good, usually starting in the mid-to-late 40s (though it can start earlier). Periods become irregular, and symptoms like hot flashes, mood swings, sleep trouble, and vaginal dryness are common as hormone levels shift.

Layered, breathable clothing, staying hydrated, cutting back on caffeine/spicy food if they trigger flashes, regular light exercise, and good sleep hygiene all help. If symptoms are seriously disrupting your life, a gynecologist can discuss options including hormone therapy — this is a medical conversation worth having, not something to just endure quietly.

Postpartum (after delivery)

Physically: bleeding for a few weeks, soreness, and fatigue are expected as your body recovers. Emotionally, mild mood dips ("baby blues") in the first two weeks are common and usually pass on their own. Rest when the baby rests, accept help with housework, and don't expect your body or energy to "bounce back" on any fixed timeline.

If sadness, hopelessness, anxiety, or a lack of connection to the baby lasts more than two weeks, or feels heavy and constant rather than up-and-down, it may be postpartum depression — a real, common, treatable medical condition, not a personal failing. Please tell your doctor, partner, or family; you deserve support, and it can get significantly better with the right help.

Contraception basics

Condoms — easy access, also protect against infections. Daily contraceptive pills — very effective if taken consistently, need a doctor's guidance to pick the right one. IUD/Copper-T — inserted by a doctor, works for years, low daily effort. Injectables/implants — longer-acting, doctor-administered. Emergency contraception — a backup for one-off situations, not a regular method. The "right" one depends on your health, lifestyle, and whether you want children later — this is genuinely worth a private conversation with a gynecologist rather than guessing.

Government support & financial help

Yes. Janani Suraksha Yojana (JSY) gives a cash incentive to women who deliver in a government or accredited hospital, with the exact amount depending on your state and rural/urban location. Pradhan Mantri Matru Vandana Yojana (PMMVY) gives an additional maternity benefit, typically for a first pregnancy. Ayushman Bharat (PM-JAY) offers cashless hospital treatment for eligible low-income families. Your local ASHA worker or nearest government hospital/PHC can help you register — amounts and eligibility rules change by state, so confirm current figures at nhm.gov.in or with your ASHA worker rather than relying on any fixed number.

Let's clear the air

Myths vs Facts

Tap a card to flip it and see the truth behind what you've probably heard.

Myth

Periods are "impure" and you shouldn't enter a kitchen or temple.

Fact

Menstrual blood is simply the shedding of the uterine lining — it isn't dirty or impure. Temple entry is a personal or cultural choice, not a health requirement.

Myth

You can't get pregnant the first time you have sex.

Fact

Pregnancy can happen the very first time, any time there's unprotected contact around ovulation. There's no "safe" first time.

Myth

PCOD/PCOS means you'll never be able to have children.

Fact

Most women with PCOD conceive naturally or with medical support like ovulation induction. It can make it take longer — it doesn't mean "never."

Myth

Exercising during pregnancy can hurt the baby.

Fact

Gentle, doctor-approved exercise is actually linked to easier labour and better outcomes. Complete bed rest is only advised for specific medical reasons.

Myth

Eating certain foods during pregnancy decides the baby's gender.

Fact

A baby's sex is determined at conception by chromosomes, not by diet. No food changes it — and in India, checking or trying to influence it is also illegal under the PCPNDT Act.

Myth

A C-section means you "failed" at giving birth.

Fact

A C-section is a medical decision made for safety, not a measure of strength or motherhood. Both paths bring a baby into the world just as validly.

Periods care

Getting Through the Hard Days, Gently

For the cramps, the mood dips, and everything in between.

🔥

Easing heavy period pain

  • Hold a warm water bottle or heating pad on your lower belly or back
  • Gentle walking or stretching — lying still can sometimes make cramps feel worse
  • A warm bath or shower relaxes tight pelvic muscles
  • A cup of warm ajwain, ginger, or chamomile water can help
  • Light massage with warm oil on the lower abdomen, in slow circles
  • Over-the-counter pain relief (like a basic anti-inflammatory) is fine occasionally — but if pain is severe every month, please see a gynecologist to rule out conditions like endometriosis
😮‍💨 better to limit
🚫

What to go easy on

  • Excess caffeine (coffee, cola) — can worsen cramps and irritability
  • Very salty or processed food — increases bloating
  • Too much sugar — can intensify mood swings after the initial lift
  • Alcohol and smoking — worsen cramps and hormonal balance
  • Extremely cold food/drinks for some people — if you notice they worsen your cramps, skip them during this week
✅ good to add
🥗

What actually helps

  • Iron-rich foods — spinach, dates, jaggery, pomegranate (you're losing blood, replace it)
  • Warm fluids — soups, herbal teas, warm water through the day
  • Bananas and dark chocolate — genuinely help with cramps and mood, not just comfort food myths
  • Enough sleep — periods hit harder on broken sleep
  • Magnesium-rich foods — nuts, seeds, and bananas can ease cramping
🎯 the actual toolkit

3 Ways to Kill Cramps, Fast

Tap-and-try techniques — pick whichever fits where you are right now.

Heat or cold, alternated

Warm compress on the lower belly for 15 min eases cramps by relaxing muscle; a cold pack on the lower back for a few minutes can numb sharper pain. Alternate if one alone isn't enough.

Child's pose & cat-cow

Two minutes of child's pose, then slow cat-cow stretches, gently mobilizes the lower back and pelvis — often cuts cramp intensity within minutes.

Sip something warm

Ajwain (carom seed) water, ginger tea, or cinnamon tea — all have mild anti-inflammatory, muscle-relaxing effects. Sip slowly over 10 minutes.

Pregnancy care

Carrying a Life, Taking Care of Yours Too

Movement, food, and everyday choices for a healthier nine months.

🧘‍♀️

Safe movement

  • 20–30 min gentle walk daily
  • Prenatal yoga or stretching
  • Pelvic floor (kegel) exercises
  • Swimming, if you're already comfortable in water
  • Avoid: contact sports, heavy weights, lying flat on the back after month 4, anything with fall risk
Eat well
🍲

What to eat more of

  • Leafy greens, dal, jaggery — for iron
  • Milk, curd, til/sesame — for calcium
  • Eggs, paneer, sprouts — for protein
  • Seasonal fruit and lots of water
  • Doctor-prescribed folic acid, iron & calcium supplements — don't skip these
Better to avoid
⚠️

What to steer clear of

  • Raw or undercooked eggs, meat, and unpasteurised dairy
  • Papaya (raw/semi-ripe) and pineapple in large amounts
  • Excess caffeine — keep it minimal
  • Alcohol and smoking — avoid entirely
  • High-mercury fish (like certain large sea fish)
  • Unwashed fruits/vegetables and street-side raw salads
🚶‍♀️

Walking & daily rhythm

Two short walks (morning and evening) are usually easier on the body than one long one. Walk on even ground, wear comfortable footwear, and always carry water. Stop and rest if you feel breathless, dizzy, or notice tightening in your belly.

🤱

Preparing for a normal delivery

Staying active, practising slow breathing daily, attending antenatal/birth-prep classes, and keeping realistic expectations all help. But remember — however your baby arrives, vaginally or via C-section, you will have done the hardest, bravest thing. There is no "better" way to become a mother.

🎯 pain & discomfort, eased

Common Aches — And What Actually Helps

Pregnancy discomfort is real. Here's what to do about the big three.

Back pain

Sleep on your side with a pillow between your knees and one supporting your belly. A warm (not hot) compress on the lower back and gentle cat-cow stretches both help through the day.

Swollen feet & ankles

Elevate your legs above heart level for 15–20 minutes a few times a day, avoid standing for long stretches, stay hydrated, and go easy on salty snacks. Sudden or one-sided swelling needs a doctor's check.

Heartburn & acidity

Eat smaller, more frequent meals instead of large ones, skip spicy/fried food in the evening, and stay upright for at least 30 minutes after eating. Ask your doctor before taking any antacid.

Mind & calm

For the Quiet Moments

Pregnancy comes with a lot of waiting, worrying, and wondering. Here's what can help your mind feel as cared for as your body.

📺

Gentle web series

  • Comfort sitcoms (Friends, The Big Bang Theory)
  • Light family dramas over intense thrillers
  • Nature/travel documentaries — calming visuals, no plot stress
  • Old, familiar favourites you can half-watch while resting
🎙️

Podcasts worth a listen

  • Pregnancy & birth-prep podcasts by certified doulas or OB-GYNs
  • Calm storytelling or "sleep story" style podcasts
  • Light comedy podcasts for a mood lift on hard days
  • Guided meditation and breathing audio series
🕉️

Mantras & quiet rituals

  • Om chanting or the Gayatri Mantra, softly, for 5–10 minutes
  • Simple affirmations: "My body knows how to do this"
  • Slow belly-breathing (inhale 4 counts, exhale 6 counts)
  • A short daily gratitude note — even one line
🎵

Songs for calm

  • Soft instrumental or lo-fi playlists for background calm
  • Classical ragas known for a soothing effect (evening ragas work well)
  • Gentle devotional bhajans, if that brings you peace
  • Old melodic Bollywood songs that feel like a hug
📖

Reading for the mind

  • Light fiction or short stories over heavy, tense plots
  • A trusted pregnancy week-by-week guide
  • Journaling — even three lines a day about how you feel
  • Letters to your baby, to read together someday
💛

If worry feels heavy

  • Talk to your partner, mother, or a close friend — don't carry it silently
  • Persistent sadness, anxiety, or hopelessness deserves a conversation with your doctor — it's common and treatable, not a weakness
  • Antenatal classes often connect you with other women going through the same thing, at the same time

Shree Sakhi was built on one simple belief: a girl or woman should never have to sit alone with a scary question about her own body. This platform brings together plain-language answers, real government resources, and a judgement-free space — so the next generation doesn't have to overthink in silence the way many of us did.
