Your First Period: Everything You Actually Need to Know

8 min read · Reviewed for accuracy · Shree Sakhi Editorial

Getting your first period — called menarche — is one of those moments that can feel confusing, scary, or even embarrassing if nobody's really explained it to you beforehand. So let's talk about it plainly, like a friend would.

When does it usually happen?

Most girls get their first period somewhere between ages 9 and 15, with 12 being fairly typical in India. There's a wide normal range — getting it earlier or later than your friends doesn't mean anything is wrong with you. Genetics, nutrition, and overall health all play a role in timing.

What does it actually feel like?

For many girls, the very first sign is just noticing a brownish or red stain in their underwear — it doesn't always start dramatically. You might feel mild cramping, a dull ache in your lower belly, or nothing unusual at all. Some girls feel more tired or emotional in the day or two before it starts, which is completely normal.

What do I actually do when it happens?

  • Use a pad first. Pads are the easiest to start with — stick the adhesive side to your underwear, change it every 4-6 hours regardless of how "full" it looks.
  • Keep a spare in your bag. A small pouch with one pad and a spare pair of underwear removes most of the anxiety about "what if it happens at school."
  • Tell a trusted adult. A mother, older sister, aunt, or teacher — you don't have to figure this out completely alone, and most women remember exactly how confusing their own first time was.

Is the pain normal?

Mild cramping is common and normal. A warm water bottle on your lower belly, gentle walking, and rest usually help. If pain is severe enough to stop you from standing up, or comes with vomiting or fainting, that's worth mentioning to a doctor — most period pain is manageable, but it shouldn't be unbearable.

Will my cycle be regular right away?

Almost never, and that's expected. It's common for the first 1-2 years of periods to be irregular — some months might be light, some heavy, some might skip entirely. Your body is still calibrating its hormones. If by 2-3 years in things are still very unpredictable, or if periods haven't started at all by age 16, that's a good time to see a doctor, but there's no need to worry in the meantime.

What if I'm scared or embarrassed?

That's an incredibly common feeling, and it fades. Every single adult woman around you went through exactly this once. There's nothing dirty, shameful, or "impure" about a normal, healthy bodily process — it's simply a sign that your body is growing exactly as it should.

This article is educational and general in nature. For specific concerns about your own periods, please talk to a trusted adult and a qualified doctor.
